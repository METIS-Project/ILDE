<h1><a name=h.qvahxbv2llx1></a><b><font size=5 color=red face=Arial><span
lang=EN-GB style='font-size:18.0pt;line-height:115%;color:red'><span
style='line-height:115%'>Name</span></span></font></b></h1>

<p class=MsoNormal><i><font size=2 color=black face=Arial><span lang=EN-GB
style='font-size:11.0pt;line-height:115%;font-style:italic'>Design patterns are
intended to be used as part of a pattern language, as an infrastructure for
design-level conversation. As such, the name of a pattern should be amenable
for use as a noun in such a conversation. It should be short, memorable and
indicative of the big idea of the pattern.</span></font></i></p>

<h1><a name=h.5yzmzrtpdxrr></a><a name=h.z1viddwjtksy></a><b><font size=5
color=red face=Arial><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:18.0pt;line-height:115%;
color:red'><span style='line-height:115%'>Problem</span></span></font></b></h1>

<p class=MsoNormal><i><font size=2 color=black face=Arial><span lang=EN-GB
style='font-size:11.0pt;line-height:115%;font-style:italic'>The problem
description provides a clear and succinct presentation of the motivation for
the pattern, its raison d'être.</span></font></i></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><i><font size=2 color=black face=Arial><span lang=EN-GB
style='font-size:11.0pt;line-height:115%;font-style:italic'>The educational
research literature tends to shy away from the term “problem”, as it may
suggest a negative attitude. In the design literature, and specifically the
computer science tradition, problems are seen as opportunities for action.</span></font></i><span
lang=EN-GB> <i><span style='font-style:italic'>From a design perspective, where
there is no problem, nothing needs to be changed, no act of design is required
and consequently there are no questions for research.</span></i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><i><font size=2 color=black face=Arial><span lang=EN-GB
style='font-size:11.0pt;line-height:115%;font-style:italic'>In either case –
explain what this pattern aims to achieve.</span></font></i></p>

<h1><a name=h.cp8dltpujsvm></a><a name=h.rp2ab0xev1s0></a><b><font size=5
color=red face=Arial><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:18.0pt;line-height:115%;
color:red'><span style='line-height:115%'>Context</span></span></font></b></h1>

<p class=MsoNormal><i><font size=2 color=black face=Arial><span lang=EN-GB
style='font-size:11.0pt;line-height:115%;font-style:italic'>A fundamental
premise of design is that problems and solutions are rarely universal. The
scope of any statement needs to be qualified if it is to be meaningful. Note
the material, social and intentional characteristics of the setting in which
the problem is embedded. These are the things you can’t change.</span></font></i></p>

<h1><a name=h.7q6ee1gpwilr></a><a name=h.b11t0notn161></a><b><font size=5
color=red face=Arial><span lang=EN-GB style='font-size:18.0pt;line-height:115%;
color:red'><span style='line-height:115%'>Solution</span></span></font></b></h1>

<p class=MsoNormal><i><font size=2 color=black face=Arial><span lang=EN-GB
style='font-size:11.0pt;line-height:115%;font-style:italic'>The solution is the
centrepiece of the pattern. In scientific terms, it is the claim that under
certain conditions the described actions will have a particular effect which
addresses the problem.</span></font></i></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><i><font size=2 color=black face=Arial><span lang=EN-GB
style='font-size:11.0pt;line-height:115%;font-style:italic'>The solution would
ideally be articulated at a level of detail which allows immediate
implementation, and yet is applicable beyond the specific experiences from
which it is derived. </span></font></i></p>
